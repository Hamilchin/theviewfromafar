My writing philosophy is simple: write often, write wrong (often), and expose ideas, especially the ones you most believe, to others.

Ideas are antifragile. They need exposure to critique to become stronger. Trying to break an idea is one of the best things you can do to it.

What writing style should I adopt?

So far, I've trained to be pretentious and perhaps too formal. This is what the pull of writing english essays does to a fella. It doesn't help that I read many old books (60+ y/o) that probably could say what they meant in 50% of the space.

I do this because that's what the people I read sound like. The "informal" writing I consume too often comes in informal settings; advertisements, social media, and the like. As such, I've tended to associate "truthy" writing with a particular style. But I don't think it's as efficient as it could be. It doesn't really match the cadence of my "internal monologue", and I've succumbed to the temptation to make flashily worded sentences even when they are harder to understand.

My main worry is that people have a similar unspoken bias to writing that is worded succinctly. I know that I certainly do, to some degree. And I'm sure many philosophers do, given how verbose many are.

Another worry is that I'm adopting a cold tone. I certainly don't mean to, but it's difficult to be warm without being informal, I've found. maybe i should start writing things without capital letters. i've seen some other people do this. how cutesy.

Am I writing for myself, or for others?

I'm afraid that there is a difference between the two. Notes to self assume a working memory of the relevant information. Public posts do not, if you want to make it a useful experience for the reader. If we only consider writer-side information, I have a suspicion that you can cut away at any piece of knowledge until it is the most bare-bones expression of the relevant idea. These are notes to self; something very close to pure thought. Yet there is something to be said about the transition from ideas in head to ideas in the open; Feynman, possibly the best lecturer of all time, famously said something about how you can't say you understand something without being able to explain it to others (or something like that). It's surprisingly hard to go from thought to coherent writing: you discover sometimes that there are holes in your argument, or that there are gaps that you have not fully understood. I write a little more about this in [The Benefits of Writing].
